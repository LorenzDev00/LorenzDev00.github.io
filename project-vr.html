TL;DR

A VR escape experience built in Unity for a university course exam. I designed a failing spaceship interior with diegetic guidance, colour-coded navigation and an atmospheric soundscape.

Learned from professor feedback that immersion breaks when any single layer is missing.

CONTEXT

This was the exam project for a VR Design course during my master's degree. The theme was easy: it wasen't required to built a complex VR; placeholder shapes were fine.
But I didn't join the course to just serve a characterless VR.

I wanted to integrate atmosphere, soundscape and environmental storytelling ... and I did.

It was also my first serious encounter with Unity; it was the occascion to learn a Game Engine and understand how events like gravity, collisions and triggers, work in a digital reality.

Programming was the foundation but I also had to understand a VR can be immersive from a user prospective.

The project had real constraints:

  • Solo development across 3 months
  • No VR headset available: all testing done in desktop mode
  • Two scenes required, completable in 5 to 10 minutes to avoid cybersickness
  • Free assets only
  • Instructions had to exist within the VR itself
  • Final evaluation by three professors using an actual headset

THE ENVIRONMENT

A spaceship was the only choice for a science fiction fan. The concept drew inspiration from Dead Space and Alien Isolation: not for horror, but for the atmospheric logic of a confined, degraded space that feels like it's running out of time.

The ship is damaged and empty. The damage creates urgency and the environment signals danger without needing a countdown timer. The emptiness was partly a practical decision at my stage of learning, but it also served the atmosphere: isolation amplifies tension far more effectively than enemies would have.

Rooms were designed to be spatially legible. Every path communicated its status through environmental cues:

  • OPEN PATHSclear floor space, functioning lights, green hologram markers
  • BLOCKED PATHSBlocked paths crates and debris physically obstructing doorways
  • DANGEROUS AREASfire, flickering lights, circuit flares signalling structural failure
  • LOCKED DOORSred warning signs and alarm lighting indicating no entry

The colour logic emerged naturally. Green meant safe, functioning, go. Red meant danger, broken, find another route. Players could read the environment without reading text.

GUIDANCE SYSTEM

The brief required instructions to exist within the VR. Most projects solved this with a flat instruction panel at the start. I used it as a storytelling opportunity instead.

The captain left hologram messages scattered across the ship; breadcrumbs guiding the crew toward the escape pods. Each message appeared at a decision point, telling the player exactly what to do next in that specific location. It solved the orientation problem without breaking immersion: the guidance was diegetic, embedded in the world, consistent with the fiction.

The professors flagged that holograms alone weren't sufficient: directional signage throughout the corridors would have reinforced the guidance system. They were right. In most sci-fi environments, lighting and signage work together to direct movement. The holograms were the right idea, but needed environmental support to complete the system.

LAYOUT

The ship used Y-junction corridors rather than a single straight path. This created genuine navigation choices: players had to read the environment to decide which direction to take. Combined with the colour-coded door system and hologram messages, the spatial layout became part of the puzzle rather than just a container for it.

The top-down view of the ship shows the branching structure clearly: multiple routes, dead ends, and the escape pods positioned at the periphery of the layout.

TESTING & FEEDBACK

The final evaluation involved three professors experiencing the VR with an actual headset: the first time any headset was used, since none were available during development. This created unavoidable issues.

  • Some walls could be passed through: collision detection gaps not visible in desktop testing
  • Desktop-to-headset input mapping created control inconsistencies nobody could have anticipated without hardware access
  • The teleportation system worked but felt wrong: instant point-to-point movement broke presence. A fluid transition would have been significantly better
  • Professors noticed the absence of footsteps and blaster sounds, but appreciated the existing soundscape: alarms, explosions, emergency alerts
  • Hologram guidance was praised but flagged as incomplete without directional environmental cues throughout the corridors

The hardware constraint was the most honest lesson: you cannot validate a VR experience without testing in the target medium. Designing blind introduced problems that no amount of desktop testing could have revealed.

LEARNINGS

Building this experience taught me that immersion is fragile. In VR especially, every missing element like a sound, a transition, a navigation cue can break the illusion faster than any visual flaw could. Good assets, a considered soundscape, spatial logic and coherent storytelling aren't optional layers. They're the structure the experience depends on.

It also introduced me to Unity, which made the development stage of my thesis game significantly faster.
More importantly, it gave me a felt understanding of what players need to feel present in a virtual environment, which became the research question research question I spent six months investigating.
