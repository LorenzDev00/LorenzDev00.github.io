TL;DR

I designed and built a custom game to investigate how different audio configurations affect player immersion.

Testing 14 participants across three sound conditions, I found that diegetic and non-diegetic sound are not interchangeable: each serves a distinct function.

Together they complete each other. Remove either layer and the illusion breaks.

THE QUESTION

Videogames exist inside a screen. The world is framed, contained, technically artificial. And yet ...

put on headphones, turn up the volume, and something shifts.
The world stops feeling hollow. It starts feeling real enough to inhabit.

I wanted to understand ... how.
How does sound create the illusion of presence in a world that doesn't exist?
How can a player's body be at a desk while their mind is somewhere else entirely?

That question became my master's thesis.

WHAT I BUILT

The lab where I had my Internship had no games, no budget for licensed tools, and no adequate testing space.

So I decided to build it myself, and this gave me something unexpected: I stopped thinking like a player and started thinking like a designer.
Instead of asking does this feel good? I was asking what could I do to make this feel good?
That shift mattered.

The game was a 2.5D medieval siege platformer in Unity: 2D gameplay mechanics inside a full 3D environment.
The 2.5D approach was a deliberate decision: it simplified development significantly while enabling full spatial distribution of audio across three dimensions, which was essential for the research.

It needed to explain itself without instructions. No tutorial screens, no text popups. Walls blocked wrong paths, climbable structures afforded jumping, an adapted version of WASD standard keyboard control scheme and the environment communicated everything.
The instruction sheet left beside the station went untouched.

To prevent players from relying on memory across three sessions, each scenario featured a completely different environmental layout while keeping the same medieval siege theme and gameplay mechanics.

The game served a dual purpose: for the players, it was meant to be entertainment; for me, it was a tool to study the nature of sound immersion.

RESEARCH DESIGN

Game audio operates on two distinct layers:

  • DIEGETIC SOUND footsteps, sword clashes, environmental noise
  • NON-DIEGETIC SOUND music and interface feedback that exists outside the game world

To understand how each layer contributes to immersion, I needed to isolate them and then test them together. Three gameplay conditions followed naturally:

  • Condition A Full soundscape (diegetic + non-diegetic)
  • Condition B Non-diegetic only (music, no environmental sounds)
  • Condition C Diegetic only (environmental sounds, no music)

Every participant experienced all three conditions: a within-subjects design that maximised sensitivity with a small sample while controlling for individual differences. Gameplay conditions order was randomised across participants to reduce bias.

EXPERIMENTAL LOOP

The experiment ran as an autonomous loop built directly into the game. Each cycle followed the same sequence:

  1. Fixation cross: 90 seconds first instance, 30 seconds subsequently
  2. Pre-game interface: 10 second countdown to prepare attention
  3. Five minutes of gameplay
  4. Post-game interface: questionnaire completion, participant-controlled pace
  5. Repeat for next condition

Building the data collection directly into the game eliminated researcher intervention between conditions and gave participants full control of their own pace between sessions.

RECRUITMENT

Participants were recruited through a two-stage process.

  1. An online screening form filtered for minimum gaming experience, habitual audio use, and comfort with earphones: 16 responses, 2 excluded, 14 participants confirmed.
  2. Eligible participants booked sessions via Google Calendar appointment slots across four days.

14 is a small sample. The findings are directional, not statistically conclusive.
But the game worked: multiple participants asked if they could keep playing after the session ended.
For a first game built from scratch, that felt like meaningful validation.

FINDING

I expected to find that some sounds mattered more than others. What I found instead was that no sound type works in isolation and the data revealed a genuinely interesting tension.

→ But produced less overall immersion than the full combination

Players responded more to environmental sounds on an immediate visceral level

→ But needed music to complete the emotional picture

DATA HIGHLIGHTS

SOUND ELEMENT PERCEPTION

  • Environmental sounds rated most immersive element overall (4.4/5)
  • 85.7% noticed the absence of diegetic sounds · 64.3% found it actively bothersome

FULL SOUNDSCAPE

  • Highest immersion (3.43)
  • Flow (3.46)
  • Positive affect (3.25)
  • Lowest negative affect (1.89)

The most complete and emotionally rewarding experience across all measures.

DIEGETIC ONLY

  • Highest arousal (3.50)
  • Liking (3.64)
  • Lower immersion than full condition

Players responded most viscerally but without music, something felt missing.

NON-DIEGETIC ONLY

  • Lowest immersion (3.07)
  • Highest negative affect (2.36)

Music alone created atmosphere but left players spatially unanchored.

PLAYERS OPINIONS

"The sounds were well made — when you are far from combat and approach, the volume increases, and when you move away it decreases."
"Particularly annoying. Being a gamer, not hearing the character's sounds made me feel out of context and not at all involved."
"This was the perfect mix. The music gave a sense of frenzy and urgency typical of battle, while the sounds emitted by the character were ideal to identify with the King."

KEY FINDING

Diegetic sound lets you hear what you see.

Non-diegetic sound lets you feel what you see.

Remove either layer and the illusion breaks.

REFLECTIONS

The experimental setup had real limitations. The room housing the experimental setup was not acoustically isolated, and the type of headphones affected comfort for some participants. In a more rigorous setup, equipment compatibility would be resolved before testing.

The within-subjects design was the right call for a small sample, but it introduced order effects. Participants didn't evaluate each condition in isolation: they evaluated it relative to what came before.

"In the second session I noticed the absence of background music because I had it in the previous session."

A between-subjects design would eliminate that bias at the cost of requiring significantly more participants. A mixed design might be the right balance.

If I ran this again:

  • Better hardware and equipment compatibility testing before sessions
  • Larger sample size for stronger statistical confidence
  • Questionnaire instruments that explicitly account for condition order

LEARNINGS

I learned that creating video games means building a complex well-orchestrated, functional system, while also designing a fully all-embracing experience in which the player can truly become absorbed.

Thanks to a previous project in which I built a Sci-Fi VR experience, I learned that good design explains itself.
I included an instruction sheet, but nobody used it. Players discovered the mechanics through the environment, and the adapted WASD layout was immediately clear: there was no second-guessing which key to press.

Discoverability wasn't a design principle I applied consciously, it was something I learned by watching players not need my instructions.
